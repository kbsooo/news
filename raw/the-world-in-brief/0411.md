Iran said it would join talks with America in Pakistan on Saturday only if its overseas assets are unfrozen and a ceasefire holds in Lebanon. Israel and Lebanon are expected to hold talks in Washington next week, a rare meeting between countries with no diplomatic ties. Disputes over whether Lebanon is covered by the truce risk derailing diplomacy; Israeli strikes have killed more than 300 people since Wednesday.

America’s annual inflation rate reached its highest level since May 2024 in March, rising to 3.3%, up from 2.4% in February. The jump was driven by surging petrol prices caused by the war in Iran. The core rate, which strips out volatile food and energy prices, rose only slightly, suggesting that the impact of the war is yet to spread through the economy.

The S&P 500 dipped on Friday as investors grew cautious ahead of talks between America and Iran, but the index ended the week up 3.6%, its best performance since November. Oil prices fell below $97 a barrel on the day. Meanwhile, two-year Treasury yields rose as stronger inflation data led investors to expect fewer interest-rate cuts.

Colombia said it would impose 100% tariffs on imports from Ecuador, matching duties introduced by its neighbour. The move deepens a growing rift between Presidents Gustavo Petro and Daniel Noboa. On Thursday Ecuador increased levies citing Colombia’s failure to tackle border security, corruption and drug trafficking, prompting Colombia to retaliate after efforts to resolve the dispute broke down.

Ukraine said it struck offshore drilling platforms in the Caspian Sea owned by Lukoil, one of Russia’s largest oil companies. The attacks, nearly 1,000km from the front line, come ahead of a brief planned ceasefire during Orthodox Easter, which will supposedly start on Saturday. Ukraine has stepped up strikes on Russia’s energy sites, which fund the war through oil and gas exports.

TSMC, a Taiwanese chipmaker, reported a 35% rise in revenue for the first quarter of 2026, compared with a year earlier. Sales, including those for AI chips used by Nvidia and Apple, grew by 45% in March. Despite higher energy costs arising from the Iran war, analysts expect demand to hold. TSMC’s shares have increased by more than 25% so far this year.

Stefano Gabbana, co-founder of the Italian fashion house Dolce & Gabbana, stepped down as chairman of the brand after over 40 years, though he will retain a creative role. The firm, which is famous for its leopard-print designs, is struggling: it has around €450m ($528m) in debt, while sales have dwindled as people splash out less on luxury goods, particularly in China.

The state of the strait
Since the ceasefire was announced on Tuesday, there has been little change in the number of ships braving the Strait of Hormuz, the international waterway that Iran has all but closed. In the absence of a formal published truce—and given that America, Iran and Israel cannot agree on what the current one covers—most vessels are biding their time. Iran announcing that it would continue striking foreign vessels in retaliation for Israeli attacks on Lebanon didn’t help.

A mere handful of ships are making it through—and some only at great expense. The Islamic Republic is using its new-found control of the strait to charge a toll: ships that are not operated by Iran or its friends must pay up to $2m (for the largest oil tankers). How far Iran will try to maintain a similar scheme under a permanent peace agreement is unclear. The idea would have seemed outlandish before this war. But on Wednesday Donald Trump said: “We’re thinking of doing it as a joint venture.”

A scrumptious run for the Red Roses
Can anyone stop England’s all-conquering Red Roses? The women’s rugby union team have won 33 consecutive matches, including the World Cup final in September. They have also been the victors of the last seven iterations of the Six Nations, an annual contest between England, France, Ireland, Italy, Scotland and Wales. The Red Roses’ first match of the 2026 tournament is against Ireland on Saturday.

The Roses have recently had to make important changes: three players are pregnant, including Zoe Stratford, the captain. Emily Scarratt, who has scored more points for England than anyone else, and star winger Abby Dow both retired in 2025. But the English have an extraordinary talent pool to draw from. Centre Meg Jones is an experienced substitute for Ms Stratford as captain. Claudia Moloney-MacDonald, a winger who can also play scrum-half, will replace Ms Dow. Ms Moloney-MacDonald will also face a very familiar opponent: her wife, Cliodhna, will make her 50th appearance for Ireland.

Sakura season comes to Washington
In 1912, the first lady of the United States and the wife of Japan’s ambassador to America planted a pair of cherry trees on the north bank of the Tidal Basin in Washington, DC, right next to the National Mall. Today such trees line the 107-acre Basin, and Washington, like Japan, celebrates with blossom festivals.

This weekend is the Japan-America society’s festival, Sakura Matsuri, held on Capitol Hill; it claims to be “the largest celebration of Japanese culture” in America. More sedate Japanophiles attendees can attend cultural demonstrations, including Taiko drumming and sumo wrestling. The festival will also feature J-Pop performances and a “Japan Smart Infrastructure Pavilion”, guaranteed to drive Americans who like fast trains and clean, functional commuter-rail networks into paroxysms of jealousy. All this conviviality will be a welcome change from recent bilateral tensions. Earlier this week Donald Trump complained that Japan had not done enough to help America in its war against Iran.

Peter Magyar, the man who might unseat Viktor Orbán
At a recent rally in Pásztó, a small town in northern Hungary, Peter Magyar drew a sizable crowd as he campaigned for local candidates from Tisza, the party he leads. Since emerging onto the political scene in early 2024, Mr Magyar has spent months touring the country and building his organisation from scratch. Tall, handsome and charismatic, he projects a modern image that contrasts with Hungary’s more traditional political class.

The 45-year-old has established himself as the most serious challenger to Viktor Orbán, who has dominated Hungarian politics for more than a decade and a half. Mr Magyar first gained attention by posting videos accusing Fidesz, the ruling party, of corruption, including attempts to cover up a child-abuse scandal. Within months, Tisza won roughly 30% of the vote in elections to the European Parliament.

A trained lawyer, Mr Magyar has worked in Berlin, Hamburg and Brussels. He used to move within Fidesz’s political orbit, holding roles linked to Hungary’s representation in the European Union. That insider status gives him direct exposure to how power is exercised at the top of government and lends credibility to his accusations of corruption—something earlier opposition figures, more easily cast as outsiders, struggled to achieve.

His break with Fidesz also appears to have been shaped by personal ambition. Party insiders had long viewed him as ill-suited for senior roles, and he never secured the prominence he sought within the system. Some also suggest that his frustration was sharpened by the greater political success of his ex-wife, Judit Varga, a former justice minister and close ally of Mr Orbán.

In policy terms, Mr Magyar offers less of a clean break with the status quo than his rhetoric suggests. Tisza’s programme remains broadly centre-right, with positions on migration and economic policy that overlap with those of Fidesz. The clearest distinction lies in foreign policy: Mr Magyar has pledged to repair relations with the EU and unlock frozen funds, which would provide relief to Hungary’s struggling economy. At home, his campaign has focused more on governance than ideology—highlighting corruption, weak economic performance and the deterioration of public services such as health care.

For many voters, support for Mr Magyar is driven less by conviction than by exhaustion with Mr Orbán’s long rule. Whether that is enough to unseat him on Sunday’s election will depend on how many Hungarians are willing, finally, to vote for change.
