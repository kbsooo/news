# Earth and Moon, then and now

As they passed over the far side of the Moon for the fourth time, on Christmas Eve 1968, the crew of Apollo 8 changed their spacecraft's orientation—and seconds later they saw, for the first time, the Earth rising above the grey cratered plains below. Nothing had prepared them for the experience. They abandoned their tasks and jostled at the windows, where, by catching the moment on colour film, pilot Bill Anders made "Earthrise" (above) an icon.

Its new counterpart, a picture of the Earth setting behind the Moon that was taken on April 6th, was a far less spontaneous creation. Artemis II's trajectory gave its crew only one chance at such a shot; but their distance from the Moon, far greater than Apollo 8's, allowed them to take their time. What's more, NASA had created pre-visualisations of what would be visible, and when, to guide them. None of that makes the recapitulation any less magnificent. ■
