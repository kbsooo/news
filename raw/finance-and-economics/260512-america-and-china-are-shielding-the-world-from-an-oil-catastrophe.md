# America and China are shielding the world from an oil catastrophe

But profiting from it is another matter

Ten weeks into the Iran war, the great oil-market mystery is deepening. Every day the Strait of Hormuz remains closed, nearly 14m barrels of oil—14% of global output—are lost. Production and shipping lags mean at least 2bn barrels will probably disappear from this year's total even if the strait reopens today. And it won't: talks between America and Iran are going nowhere. Yet Brent crude fetches just $107 a barrel. That is well below the $129 it hit in 2022, after Russia's invasion of Ukraine, and nowhere near the $150-200 analysts had predicted in the event that the Iran war dragged on.

One reason is that oil traders remain perpetually optimistic about a diplomatic breakthrough. "Front-month" Brent—the nearest futures contract and global benchmark—locks in a price for oil to be loaded onto tankers in roughly two months' time. Every few days Donald Trump, America's president, hints that a resolution may be imminent, making markets reluctant to price in eight more weeks of disruption. But lately something else has been going on. Even spot prices have calmed: "dated" Brent, which tracks crude loading in the coming days, traded at a $25 premium to front-month futures in early April; now the gap is just a few dollars.

Two forces explain why the panic has faded. First, petro-powers outside the Gulf have turbocharged exports. Smaller producers have chipped in: net of imports, Canada shipped an extra 400,000 barrels per day (b/d) of crude and refined products in the four weeks to May 10th, compared with the same period in 2025. Venezuela and Norway each contributed another 200,000 b/d; Brazil added 100,000 b/d. Most remarkable, however, has been America. At nearly 9m b/d, its net petroleum exports over these four weeks were the highest in history, and 3.8m b/d above the equivalent period last year, according to Vortexa, a ship tracker.

America's export machine took a few weeks to crank up. New contracts had to be signed; extra barrels produced or drawn from reserves; pipelines booked to the coast. Storage tanks had to be freed at terminals so the right oil could be blended, linked to the right berth. In March freight rates from the Atlantic to Asia and Europe—the main destinations for American crude—jumped to lure new tankers onto those routes. To compensate buyers, the discount on West Texas Intermediate (WTI), America's flagship crude, relative to Brent and Dubai—the European and Asian benchmarks—broke records.

All this helped bring a tide of non-Gulf barrels to importers, narrowing the supply gap to roughly 8m b/d. Even more improbably, during the four weeks to May 10th the big oil-buying regions imported 11m b/d less petroleum than over that period last year. China's purchases alone dropped by a precipitous 6.6m b/d. Instead of lapping up all the crude available abroad, the country's refiners have even resold some cargoes they had pledged to buy from west Africa and beyond to other Asian buyers.

The huge drop in imports is not good news. Some of it reflects demand destruction. Crude shortages have already forced refiners across Asia and Europe to cut throughput by nearly 4m b/d. The market has also lost 4.4m b/d of refined-product exports from the Gulf, pushing diesel, gasoline and jet fuel prices up by 60-120% across big markets—much higher than the 40% rise for crude between January and April. Squeezed by higher fuel costs, consumers have cut back. Many petrochemical plants, starved of naphtha, another oil product and a crucial plastics feedstock, are also running below capacity.

Yet most estimates of demand destruction fall below 5m b/d, suggesting much of the drop in imports reflects caution, rather than privation. Some buyers, too, may well believe the strait will reopen soon, prompting them to defer purchases until prices fall. The surprising result is a mini-glut of crude. After falling in March, the volume on tankers at sea rose in April—even as those of diesel, gasoline and jet fuel cratered well below five-year averages. That is keeping the Brent price down.

How long can it last? Satellite imagery of China's floating-roof tanks suggests onshore stocks have barely budged—implying refineries have slashed throughput. But crude imports have collapsed so much that something else must be going on, too. Martijn Rats of Morgan Stanley, a bank, suspects crude once held in unobservable underground caverns has migrated to overground storage, quietly covering the shortfall.

Such drawdowns of inventory are likely to accelerate. Within weeks the maintenance season for Chinese refineries will end. Soon they may increase exports of refined products, with the government having loosened a ban it introduced in early March. China holds an estimated 1.2bn barrels of crude in storage: enough, even if several million barrels are drawn per day, to keep imports depressed for much of the year. But China is unlikely to want to go that far. "Strategically, they don't want to draw down everything this year," says Neil Crosby of Sparta Commodities, a data firm. That would mean China importing more, and the rest of the world less.

A bigger problem may come from America. In March, as part of a release co-ordinated by the International Energy Agency, a club of big energy importers, the country agreed to withdraw 172m barrels from its strategic oil reserve. This has allowed it to boost crude exports by over 600,000 b/d while retaining healthy commercial stocks of its own, despite having not produced much more since the war began.

American exports are now more likely to fall than rise. As in China, maintenance season for refiners will soon end, and as they increase throughput, more than 500,000 b/d currently earmarked for export could be redirected, estimates Kpler, a data firm. More worrying, America's motor-fuel stocks are plummeting at record speed. If this continues, even a modest rise in crude prices could push petrol to $5 a gallon—a threshold last breached in 2022, when it hurt both drivers and President Joe Biden's approval ratings.

In recent weeks the Trump administration has been considering a ban on refined-product export, to avoid similar damage. When Brent hovered around $100, one insider put the odds of such a ban at 35%. Those odds are higher now—and could cross 50% if pump prices jump by Memorial Day on May 25th, according to several sources. That would roil energy markets the world over.

Even if Mr Trump does not make things worse, oil stocks will keep falling everywhere. America and China have bought the world time. It still faces a reckoning if Hormuz stays shut. ■
